Key constraints

  • DR only publishes Mon–Fri — weekends extend the window
  • Upcoming holidays: 25 Apr (Freedom Day, Sat), 1 May (Labor Day, Fri) — both shift everything forward
  • Referenda (PM signature) is almost always same-day as promulgation — not a bottleneck

Filing window for old 5-year rule. Per TC 1133/2025,[TC] applications filed BEFORE the law takes effect are assessed under the OLD legal regime (5 years, not 10). The filing window is roughly 3–9 days between promulgation and law-in-force. However, you still need to satisfy the statutory requirements of your pathway at filing:
  • Art. 3.º (marriage, Modelo 3): 3 years of marriage at filing
  • Art. 6.º n.º 1 (residency, Modelo 6.1): 5 years legal residence at filing (with some contested early-filing arguments — see legal brief)
  • Art. 6.º n.º 8 (ascendant, Modelo 6.8): 5 years legal residence immediately preceding the request — text is unambiguous, must be complete at filing
Prepare documents now; file immediately when you (a) meet your pathway's requirements AND (b) promulgation has been announced — ideally both before law-in-force.
Promulgation signal. Watch presidencia.pt/atividade-legislativa for the promulgation announcement. Once you see "Presidente da República promulga Decreto 48/XVII", you have 3–9 days before the law is in force. That's your filing window.

Constitutionality Analysis

How the April 2026 decree addresses the four provisions struck down by TC 1133/2025. Two decrees are analyzed separately.

Decreto 48/XVII — Nationality Law (LOW RISK)

Government says "constitucionalmente robusta."[GOV] PS says issues "resolved."[3] Morais Leitão confirms TC never challenged core residency increases.[ML]

TC 1133/2025 Finding Oct 2025 Provision Apr 2026 Fix Risk
Criminal bar disproportionate Barred naturalization for anyone sentenced to ≥2 years — any crime Raised to ≥3 years, limited to enumerated serious crimes (terrorism, violent/organized crime, state security, human trafficking)[CM] LOW — narrower scope + higher threshold addresses proportionality
Retroactive residency periods New 10-year rule applied retroactively to pending cases — violated protection of trust Removed retroactive application. New rules apply only after law takes effect. Pending applications assessed under old rules (5yr).[TC] RESOLVED — directly addresses TC finding
"Fraude manifesta" unclear Consolidation of nationality didn't operate in cases of "manifest fraud" — no clear criteria distinguishing from ordinary fraud Exception revised for "third parties in good faith"[1] LOW — revised formulation
Residency clock start date Clock started from application date Clock starts from first residence card issuance[8] — TC did not flag this as unconstitutional in 1133/2025 MINIMAL — policy choice within legislative discretion, not flagged by TC
No transitional regime. The revised decree includes no grandfathering for applicants accumulating residency under the current 5-year rule. TC 1133/2025 established that pending applications are protected, but people who haven't filed yet would face the new 10-year requirement immediately. This is a political risk (and was PS's main legislative proposal[PS]), but not necessarily unconstitutional — TC did not require a transitional regime, only protection of already-filed applications.

Decreto 49/XVII — Penal Code / Loss of Nationality (HIGH RISK)

TC 1134/2025 was unanimous against the Oct 2025 version. PS says revised version "mantém muitos dos problemas" (maintains many problems).[TC2]

TC 1134/2025 Finding Oct 2025 Provision Apr 2026 Fix Risk
Violated equality (Art. 13 CRP) Loss of nationality as accessory penalty — created two classes of citizens Maintained loss of nationality but narrowed: ≥5 years imprisonment for enumerated grave crimes only HIGH — core constitutional objection (equality) not structurally resolved
Violated proportionality (Art. 18 CRP) Penalty of losing nationality disproportionate to criminal conduct Higher threshold (5yr vs 4yr) and narrower crime list MEDIUM — improved but still novel penalty; TC may re-examine
Violated citizenship dignity (Art. 26 CRP) Nationality treated as revocable privilege, not fundamental right No structural change to this framing HIGH — conceptual objection unchanged
Key distinction: Decreto 49 (Penal Code) is the one with serious constitutional risk. Decreto 48 (Nationality Law) is widely considered safe. These are separate decrees — TC challenge on Decreto 49 does NOT block Decreto 48.[4] PS's own statement confirms they consider nationality law issues "resolved" while Penal Code decree "maintains many problems."[3]

Calendar vs business days. Constitutional deadlines (20-day promulgation, 8-day fiscalizacao, 25-day TC review) count calendar days per CRP — weekends and holidays tick. But redacao final is administrative work by parliament services — only progresses on business days. Judicial deadlines (TC) are suspended from Palm Sunday to Easter Monday (29 Mar–6 Apr 2026). Easter weekend (3–5 Apr) effectively adds ~3 days to redacao final vs a normal week.
